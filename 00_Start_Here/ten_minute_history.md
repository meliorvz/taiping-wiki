# The Taiping Rebellion in Ten Minutes

The Taiping Rebellion began with a man who believed heaven had given him a mission. Hong Xiuquan, 洪秀全, Hóng Xiùquán, was a failed examination candidate from Guangdong. After reading Christian tracts and reinterpreting earlier visions, he came to believe that 上帝, Shàngdì, the supreme God, had chosen him to destroy demons and cleanse China. His friend Feng Yunshan, 冯云山, Féng Yúnshān, carried this message into Guangxi and built a religious community called the God Worshipping Society, 拜上帝会, Bài Shàngdì Huì.[^britannica-hong][^reilly]

Guangxi was already violent. Migrants, Hakka communities, local lineages, miners, militia leaders, and Qing officials struggled over land, security, and authority. The God Worshippers gave people discipline, belonging, and a story that explained their enemies as demons. By January 1851 the society had become an army. At Jintian, 金田, in Guiping County, Hong's followers rose against the Qing and began calling themselves the Taiping Army.[^acs-jintian]

The Taiping moved faster than Qing commanders could contain. They broke out of Guangxi, marched through Hunan, reached the Yangzi River, and captured Nanjing in March 1853. They renamed it Tianjing, 天京, "Heavenly Capital." From then on, the rebellion was not just a moving insurgency. It was a rival state with a capital, kings, laws, taxes, armies, official books, and sacred claims.[^britannica-taiping]

The Taiping promised a new world. Their texts denounced idol worship, opium, prostitution, and Manchu rule. The Land System of the Heavenly Dynasty, 天朝田亩制度, Tiāncháo tiánmǔ zhìdù, imagined common cultivation, public granaries, and shared use of food, clothing, and money. The promise was powerful. Wartime practice was harsher. Armies needed grain, cities needed supplies, and local people lived with requisition, flight, famine, and violence.[^tianmu]

The Taiping court contained a fatal contradiction. Hong Xiuquan was Heavenly King, but Yang Xiuqing, 杨秀清, claimed to speak with the voice of the Heavenly Father during spirit possession. That gave Yang religious and political power that threatened Hong. In 1856 Hong ordered Wei Changhui, 韦昌辉, to kill Yang. Wei killed Yang and many of his followers. Hong then killed Wei. Shi Dakai, 石达开, left the capital with his own army. The Tianjing Incident weakened the Taiping state at the moment it needed unity.[^michael]

The Taiping did not fall immediately. New commanders, especially Chen Yucheng, 陈玉成, and Li Xiucheng, 李秀成, revived the war after 1856. Hong Rengan, 洪仁玕, arrived in Tianjing in 1859 with experience from Hong Kong and treaty-port Christianity. His New Treatise on Aids to Administration, 资政新篇, proposed newspapers, railways, banks, postal offices, and systematic foreign relations. The ideas were striking, but the regime was fighting for survival.[^zizheng][^yingjie]

The Qing survived by changing how it fought. The old Banner and Green Standard forces could not defeat the Taiping by themselves. Zeng Guofan, 曾国藩, built the Xiang Army, 湘军, from Hunan networks of gentry, scholars, officers, and recruits. Li Hongzhang, 李鸿章, built the Huai Army, 淮军, for the lower Yangzi theater. These armies saved the dynasty, but they also moved real military power toward provincial commanders.[^kuhn]

Foreign intervention mattered most around Shanghai. Missionaries argued about whether the Taiping were Christian, heretical, or dangerous. Treaty-port merchants and diplomats cared about trade and security. Frederick Townsend Ward and later Charles Gordon commanded the Ever Victorious Army, 常胜军, a small foreign-officered Qing auxiliary force. It did not win the war alone, but it helped Qing forces in the Shanghai and Suzhou corridor.[^ward][^wilson]

The end came through siege. Qing forces took Anqing in 1861, cutting Tianjing's western lifeline. Chen Yucheng was captured and executed in 1862. Zeng Guoquan's army tightened the siege of Tianjing. Hong Xiuquan died in June 1864. On 19 July 1864 Qing troops broke into the city. Tianjing was looted and burned. Li Xiucheng fled, was captured, wrote his disputed self-account in Qing custody, and was executed.[^britannica-taiping][^michael]

The war killed or displaced millions. The familiar estimate of about 20 million deaths comes from demographic reconstruction, not from a body count. The best way to understand the catastrophe is to combine broad estimates with local evidence: gazetteers, genealogies, relief records, inscriptions, burial accounts, and survivor memory. The Taiping war mattered because it changed the Qing state, reshaped local society, and gave later political movements a past they kept arguing over.[^meyer]
