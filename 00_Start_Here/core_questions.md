# Core Questions

## What was the Taiping Rebellion?

The Taiping Rebellion was a war between the Qing dynasty and a rival religious kingdom founded by Hong Xiuquan. It began in Guangxi in 1851, captured Nanjing in 1853, and ended with the Qing recapture of that city in 1864. It was also a social crisis, a religious movement, and a turning point in Qing military organization.[^michael]

## How do you pronounce Taiping?

A rough English pronunciation is "tie-ping." The pinyin is Tàipíng. The word means "great peace," but the war fought under that name devastated large parts of south and central China.

## Why is Nanjing called Tianjing in this wiki?

After the Taiping captured Nanjing in 1853, they renamed it 天京, Tiānjīng, "Heavenly Capital." The new name expressed their claim that the city was no longer a Qing provincial and imperial city but the sacred capital of the Heavenly Kingdom.[^michael]

## Why did the rebellion begin?

It began where religious conviction met local insecurity. Hong supplied the sacred message. Feng Yunshan built the organization. Guangxi supplied recruits, enemies, and conflict. Qing authority could punish, but it could not easily restore trust or security in the countryside.[^reilly]

## Were the Taiping Christians?

They used Christian-derived language and texts, but the movement was not a Protestant church. Hong claimed to be Jesus's younger brother. Yang Xiuqing claimed to speak as the Heavenly Father. Taiping religion became its own Chinese sacred system, with Protestant material, Chinese language, anti-idolatry, kingship, and war bound together.[^reilly]

## Did the Taiping redistribute land?

Their Land System promised common cultivation and shared provision. The text is real and important. Evidence for systematic implementation is much weaker. Treat the land system as a powerful program and a later political symbol, not as proof that every Taiping county practiced equal redistribution.[^tianmu]

## Why did the Taiping grow so quickly?

They grew because religious discipline met local insecurity, because early Qing forces failed to contain them, and because the army moved fast once it broke out of Guangxi. River mobility after the capture of Wuchang accelerated the march to Nanjing.[^michael]

## Why did the Northern Expedition fail?

The Taiping tried to send an army toward Beijing after capturing Nanjing. The expedition became isolated far from the capital, short of supply, exposed to weather, and vulnerable to Qing counterattack. It showed ambition, but also the danger of sending unsupported forces deep into north China.[^michael]

## Who actually defeated the Taiping?

Qing provincial armies, especially the Xiang and Huai armies, did most of the decisive work. Foreign-officered forces mattered in the Shanghai-Suzhou theater but did not win the war alone.[^kuhn][^wilson]

## Why did the Taiping fail?

They failed because several pressures converged: the 1856 Tianjing Incident, weak territorial administration, failed northern strategy, dependence on supply routes, Qing provincial military adaptation, and foreign-backed operations in the lower Yangzi. No single explanation is enough.[^kuhn]

## How many people died?

No one counted the dead. The familiar figure of about 20 million deaths comes from demographic reconstruction, especially the tradition associated with Ho Ping-ti's work on Qing population history. A safer reader-facing formula is that the war killed, displaced, or removed from population registers many millions, with regional pages explaining methods and uncertainty.[^ho][^meyer]

## Why does this war matter?

The war shows that nineteenth-century China was not only a story of foreign pressure. China also endured a massive internal war that transformed the state, devastated local society, and shaped later ideas of revolution, nationalism, Christianity, and social equality.

## Notes

[^michael]: Franz H. Michael, *The Taiping Rebellion: History and Documents*, Vol. I: *History* (Seattle: University of Washington Press, 1966). Use for broad narrative and campaign sequence; check Chinese sources for final article-level claims.
[^reilly]: Thomas H. Reilly, *The Taiping Heavenly Kingdom: Rebellion and the Blasphemy of Empire* (Seattle: University of Washington Press, 2004). Use for the interpretation of Taiping religion as a Chinese religious system formed through Protestant-derived material and Chinese sacred politics.
[^tianmu]: 《天朝田亩制度》, Taiping official land and social program. This supports claims about the program's wording and ideal order, not proof of uniform implementation.
[^kuhn]: Philip A. Kuhn, *Rebellion and Its Enemies in Late Imperial China: Militarization and Social Structure, 1796-1864* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1970). Use for provincial militarization and Qing counterinsurgency structure.
[^wilson]: Andrew Wilson, *The Ever-Victorious Army: A History of the Chinese Campaign under Lt. Col. C. G. Gordon* (Edinburgh and London: William Blackwood and Sons, 1868). Near-contemporary and Gordon-centered; use with Qing and diplomatic records.
[^ho]: Ho Ping-ti 何炳棣, *Studies on the Population of China, 1368-1953* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1959). Use for demographic reconstruction traditions, not as a body count.
[^meyer]: Tobie Meyer-Fong, *What Remains: Coming to Terms with Civil War in 19th Century China* (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2013). Use for civilian suffering, burial, commemoration, and postwar memory, especially in Jiangnan.
