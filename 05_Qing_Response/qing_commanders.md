# Qing Commanders at War

The Qing victory over the Taiping was an institutional achievement — the product of new fiscal arrangements, regional army organization, and foreign assistance — but it was also a story of individual commanders. The war produced a generation of military leaders whose personal qualities, relationships, rivalries, and limitations shaped the conduct of operations and the politics of command. Some were scholar-officials who learned war in middle age; others were career soldiers who rose through the ranks. Some died in the war; others went on to dominate late Qing politics for decades. Together they constituted a military leadership class unlike any the Qing had produced since the seventeenth century.[^kuhn][^michael]

## Xiang Rong / 向荣 (c. 1792–1856)

Xiang Rong was the most prominent Qing field commander of the war's first phase. Born in Gansu to a Han Banner family, he had a long career in the Green Standard before the Taiping rising, serving in campaigns against the White Lotus and against border disturbances in the northwest. When the Taiping rose in Guangxi, Xiang Rong was among the first senior commanders dispatched to suppress them. He pursued the Taiping from Yong'an (永安) across Guangxi into Hunan, fighting a series of rearguard actions, but could not stop their relentless advance.

After Nanjing fell to the Taiping in March 1853, Xiang Rong established the Jiangnan Great Camp (江南大营, Jiāngnán Dàyíng) — a fortified encampment south of Nanjing intended to besiege the Taiping capital. For three years, Xiang's forces bottled up the Taiping in Nanjing, repelling sallies and maintaining the siege. The camp was a symbol of Qing determination and a constant threat to the Taiping capital, but it was also a position of grinding frustration: Xiang never had enough troops to assault Nanjing's towering walls, and his relationship with the court was strained by his inability to deliver victory. In June 1856, a coordinated Taiping assault under Yang Xiuqing broke the Jiangnan camp. Xiang Rong was wounded and retreated to Danyang (丹阳), where he died — possibly by suicide — in August 1856. His death marked the end of the war's first phase and left the Qing command structure in disarray.[^luo][^michael]

## He Chun / 和春 (?–1860) and Zhang Guoliang / 张国梁 (c. 1823–1860)

He Chun and Zhang Guoliang rebuilt the Jiangnan Great Camp after Xiang Rong's death, and their fates were inextricably linked. He Chun, a Manchu bannerman, was appointed imperial commissioner to command the renewed siege of Nanjing. Zhang Guoliang — originally a bandit from Guangdong who had surrendered to the Qing and risen through the ranks to become a general officer — was the most effective combat commander in the Jiangnan theater, respected by both Qing and Taiping forces for his aggressive tactics and personal courage.

The rebuilt Jiangnan camp was larger and better-equipped than Xiang Rong's original. Reestablished in 1858 after sweeping Taiping forces from the Nanjing area, it again invested the capital. But in May 1860, Li Xiucheng's eastern offensive — a brilliant strategic maneuver that marched Taiping forces through Zhejiang before doubling back to strike the camp's rear and flank — collapsed Qing defenses. Zhang Guoliang was killed covering the retreat. He Chun escaped to Danyang, where he died — possibly by suicide — within weeks. The destruction of the second Jiangnan camp demonstrated conclusively that the traditional approach of an imperial commissioner commanding Green Standard forces was fundamentally inadequate and that the future lay with the provincial armies.[^luo]

## Senggelinqin / 僧格林沁 (1811–1865)

Senggelinqin — a Mongol prince of the Khorchin Banner, a descendant of Genghis Khan's brother, and a son-in-law of the Daoguang Emperor — was the most important Manchu-Mongol commander of the era. He commanded the Qing forces that met and destroyed the Taiping Northern Expedition (1853–1855) on the North China Plain. The Northern Expeditionary force, led by Taiping commanders Lin Fengxiang and Li Kaifang, had pushed to within striking distance of Tianjin, threatening Beijing itself. Senggelinqin's cavalry — Mongol and Banner horsemen operating in the open terrain north of the Yellow River — harassed, surrounded, and ultimately crushed the Taiping force at Lianzhen (连镇) and Fengguantun (冯官屯) in 1855. It was one of the few unequivocal Qing battlefield victories of the war's early years.

Senggelinqin later commanded Qing forces against the Anglo-French expedition during the Second Opium War, notably at the Battle of Baliqiao outside Beijing on 21 September 1860, where his traditional cavalry was annihilated by British and French infantry firepower. He survived the defeat and continued to serve the dynasty against the Nian rebels, but he was killed in May 1865 when Nian forces ambushed him during a personal reconnaissance in Shandong — he was beheaded, and his body was found several days later. His death was a severe blow to Qing prestige and marked the effective end of Banner cavalry as a serious military instrument.[^michael][^luo]

## Hu Linyi / 胡林翼 (1812–1861)

Hu Linyi was, alongside Zeng Guofan, the most important architect of the Qing recovery — and he is less famous than he deserves to be. A native of Yiyang, Hunan, and a jinshi degree-holder (1836), Hu served as intendant of Guizhou before the war and then as governor of Hubei from 1855 until his death. As governor of Hubei, Hu was responsible for the war's central theater — the middle Yangzi, including the critically important city of Wuchang — and he managed it with extraordinary effectiveness.

Hu was an organizer, not a field commander. He created the fiscal and logistical system that sustained the Xiang Army's operations in Hubei and Jiangxi. He established the Hubei Arsenal to manufacture weapons and ammunition. He worked in close partnership with Zeng Guofan, whom he respected and supported without jealousy. When Zeng was criticized at court, Hu defended him; when Zeng needed reinforcements, Hu provided them. Hu's death in September 1861 — from overwork, at the age of forty-nine — was a genuine strategic loss. Zeng Guofan wrote that without Hu Linyi, the reconquest would not have been possible. Chinese historiography has honored him as one of the "Three Heroes of the Tongzhi Restoration" (同治中兴三大名臣, Tóngzhì Zhōngxīng Sān Dà Míngchén), alongside Zeng Guofan and Zuo Zongtang.[^kuhn][^luo]

## Peng Yulin / 彭玉麟 (1816–1890)

Peng Yulin was the Xiang Army's foremost naval commander and one of the most eccentric figures of the late Qing. Born in Hengyang, Hunan, to a modest scholarly family, he lost his father young and supported his mother through teaching. He joined Zeng Guofan's staff in 1853 and was given command of a naval squadron — despite having no prior naval experience. He proved to be a natural commander, with an intuitive grasp of river warfare.

Peng built and led the Xiang Navy, a fleet of war junks and gunboats that contested and ultimately won control of the Yangzi River from the Taiping. He was present at nearly every major river engagement from 1854 to 1864. His leadership style combined personal austerity — he refused promotions, titles, and high salaries, living simply among his sailors — with ferocious personal courage; he often led attacks from the front, sword in hand. Soldiers followed him because he shared their risks and their privations.

Peng's postwar career was defined by his refusals. The Qing court repeatedly offered him high provincial posts — governor-general of Liangjiang, minister of war — and he repeatedly declined them, preferring to serve as an inspector without fixed responsibilities, free to travel and investigate corruption. His most famous trait was his moral rigidity: he was known for impeaching corrupt officials regardless of rank, including former colleagues and relatives of the powerful. He also painted thousands of ink-plum-blossom (墨梅, mòméi) paintings as a lifelong act of mourning for a woman he had loved in his youth and lost. He died in 1890, one of the last of the great Taiping-era commanders, admired and slightly alien — a warrior-ascetic out of step with the politics of his age.[^luo]

## Zuo Zongtang / 左宗棠 (1812–1885)

Zuo Zongtang had one of the strangest career trajectories of any Qing commander. A native of Xiangyin, Hunan, he passed the provincial examinations as a young man but failed the metropolitan examinations three times — a failure that burned in him for life. He retreated to rural life in Hunan, where he read widely in geography, agriculture, and military strategy, cultivating the persona of a self-taught "scholar in the mountains" (山中学者, shānzhōng xuézhě).

The Taiping war pulled him out of obscurity. He became an advisor to the governor of Hunan, Luo Bingzhang (骆秉章), in 1852, and gained a reputation as the strategic brain behind Hunan's successful defense and the reorganization of provincial administration. His abrasive personality — brilliant, self-confident, contemptuous of mediocrity — made him powerful enemies, and in 1860 he was accused of overstepping his authority as a mere secretary. Zeng Guofan intervened to save him, and Zuo was given command of his own army in Zhejiang.

From 1862, Zuo commanded an independent campaign in Zhejiang province, retaking Hangzhou (1864) and clearing the Taiping from the southeastern coast. His army, built on the Xiang Army model, operated with considerable independence from Zeng Guofan's central command structure — a reflection of both Zuo's talent and his unwillingness to be anyone's subordinate. His postwar career was the most distinguished of any Taiping-era commander. He suppressed the Nian and Muslim rebellions, conquered Xinjiang from Yakub Beg's regime in a brilliant campaign across the deserts of Central Asia (1875–1877), and became the leading advocate for territorial expansion and military modernization — clashing publicly with Li Hongzhang's naval-first policy. He died in 1885, remembered as a patriot-hero and a difficult, unforgettable man.[^luo][^michael]

## Zeng Guoquan / 曾国荃 (1824–1890)

Zeng Guoquan, the younger brother of Zeng Guofan, was the Xiang Army's siege specialist. Born in Xiangxiang, Hunan, the fourth of five brothers, he joined his elder brother's staff as a young man and was given independent field command in 1856. He quickly acquired a reputation — and a nickname — that defined his military career: "Iron Helmet" (铁盔, tiěkuí), for his refusal to abandon a siege under any circumstances, regardless of casualties or political pressure.

Zeng Guoquan commanded the siege of Anqing (1860–1861), the most important Qing victory of the middle war period. He surrounded the city, constructed multiple lines of fortifications, beat off repeated Taiping relief attempts by Chen Yucheng and other commanders, and starved Anqing into surrender over eighteen months. His forces then moved downstream to Nanjing, where he commanded the final siege from May 1862 to July 1864. When Nanjing's walls were breached on 19 July 1864, Zeng Guoquan's troops entered the city and carried out a massacre — contemporaries estimated over 100,000 dead — that earned him both official honors and quiet, lasting criticism.

Zeng Guoquan's postwar career was overshadowed by his brother's reputation and by his own mixed record as a civil administrator. He served as governor of Hubei and Shaanxi, and briefly as governor-general of Liangjiang, but was undistinguished in civil posts. He died in 1890. Historians have debated whether his military achievements were the product of personal talent — his tenacity and willingness to absorb appalling casualties — or of the resources and command structure that his elder brother provided. The evidence supports both readings: he was a genuine siege specialist, but he operated within a system that Zeng Guofan built.[^luo]

## Li Xubin / 李续宾 (1818–1858)

Li Xubin was the most talented field commander the Xiang Army produced — and his death at the Battle of Sanhe (三河, Sānhé) in November 1858 was the worst Qing battlefield defeat of the middle war. Born in Xiangxiang, Hunan, the same county that produced the Zeng brothers and Luo Zenan, he joined the Xiang Army at its founding and rose rapidly through demonstrated combat skill. He fought with distinction at the recapture of Wuchang (1854) and in the Jiangxi campaigns (1855–1857), earning a reputation for aggressive, decisive action and for the fierce loyalty of his troops.

In the autumn of 1858, Li Xubin led a force of approximately 6,000 Xiang Army troops into Anhui, advancing toward Luzhou (庐州). At Sanhe, a small town on the route, he was ambushed and surrounded by a much larger Taiping force under Chen Yucheng (陈玉成) and Li Xiucheng. Li Xubin refused to retreat — some sources say he considered withdrawal dishonorable, others that terrain made escape impossible — and his entire command was destroyed. He died in the fighting; whether killed in combat or by his own hand is uncertain.

The Battle of Sanhe was a collective trauma for the Xiang Army. Some of its best officers died alongside Li Xubin, including trusted subordinates who had fought with him for years. Zeng Guofan reportedly wept when he learned the news. The defeat demonstrated that even the Xiang Army could be crushed if isolated from supply lines and outnumbered. It also spurred organizational reforms — larger formations, better coordination between columns, more careful advance — that shaped the war's later phase and ensured that no single force would again be sent so deep into enemy territory alone.[^luo][^michael]

## Debates

How much of the Qing victory was due to exceptional individuals, and how much to structural advantages — demographic weight, control of the coast, tax revenue, foreign assistance? The structural argument notes that the Qing commanded far greater resources than the Taiping and that the provincial army model was an organizational innovation that any competent official could have implemented. The individual argument counters that without the particular talents of Zeng Guofan, Hu Linyi, Zuo Zongtang, and their subordinates, the structural advantages would not have been deployed effectively. Both positions have force: the war's outcome was overdetermined, yet the specific path to victory was shaped by specific people at specific moments.

A related debate concerns the relationships among the commanders. Were the Xiang Army's generals a cohesive team united by shared regional loyalty, Confucian ideology, and personal bonds? Or were they a collection of ambitious men whose rivalries were suppressed only by wartime necessity? The evidence supports both readings. Zeng Guofan's moral authority held the group together, but tensions — between Zuo Zongtang and the Zeng brothers, between the Hunan and Anhui factions, between provincial commanders and the Manchu court — were real and persistent. The Qing victory was a collective achievement, but the collective was never fully harmonious, and the fractures visible during the war widened in the postwar decades.[^kuhn]
