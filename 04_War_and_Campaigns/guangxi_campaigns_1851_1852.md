# Guangxi Campaigns and the Yong'an Period, 1851-1852 / 廣西戰役與永安時期

The period from the Jintian Uprising in January 1851 to the Taiping army's entry into Hunan in mid-1852 transformed the movement from a localized religious society into a mobile conquering force with a formalized court structure, a calendar, a treasury, and a named pantheon of kings. The Guangxi campaigns were the Taiping's military and political nursery — a sequence of desperate escapes, improvised battles, and near-destructions from which the movement emerged hardened, tested, and unrecognizable to the Qing officials who had dismissed it as another provincial disturbance.[^michael1]

## Early engagements: Jintian and the Qing response

The Jintian Uprising on 11 January 1851 is treated in full in the Jintian Uprising page (see Read Next). What matters for the Guangxi campaigns is the military situation it created: approximately 10,000–20,000 Taiping adherents — fighters, families, and camp followers — concentrated at Jintian village in Guiping County, surrounded by Qing forces that were initially piecemeal and poorly coordinated but that grew rapidly as the seriousness of the insurrection became apparent.[^luo]

The first significant engagement came within days of the uprising declaration. A Qing detachment under Li Dianyuan (李殿元), the acting governor of Guangxi, advanced on Jintian but was ambushed and routed by Taiping forces commanded by Yang Xiuqing (杨秀清) and Xiao Chaogui (萧朝贵) at Caijiang Village (蔡江村). The Qing lost several hundred men and the Taiping captured weapons and supplies — a morale victory out of proportion to its scale, because it proved that imperial regulars could be beaten.[^michael1]

Through the spring of 1851, the Taiping remained mobile within eastern Guangxi, avoiding large Qing concentrations while absorbing new recruits from Hakka communities, miners, and bandit groups. The emperor in Beijing dismissed the Guangxi governor and appointed Saišangga (赛尚阿), a senior Manchu official who had previously served on the Grand Council, as Imperial Commissioner (钦差大臣, Qīnchāi Dàchén) with authority over all military operations in the province. Saišangga brought additional Banner troops and adopted a strategy of encirclement — identifying Taiping positions, cutting supply routes, and tightening a cordon.[^mao]

## The march to Yong'an

The summer of 1851 was a battle for survival. Qing forces, now under more competent direction, attacked Taiping camps at Guancun (官村) and other positions. The Taiping, although winning skirmishes, could not break the strategic encirclement while remaining in the Thistle Mountain (紫荆山, Zǐjīngshān) area. Yang Xiuqing, emerging as the movement's operational commander, recognized that staying in eastern Guangxi meant slow strangulation. He ordered the army to move.

In August–September 1851, Taiping forces fought through Qing lines at Guancun and marched northward. The breakout was costly — Luo Ergang records heavy casualties from rearguard actions and straggler losses — but it carried the army out of the Thistle Mountain trap and into the plains of northeastern Guangxi. The destination was the walled town of Yong'an (永安, Yǒng'ān).

On or about 25 September 1851, Taiping forces seized Yong'an. The Qing garrison, a few hundred Green Standard troops and local militia, was overwhelmed. For the first time, the Taiping held a county seat — not a village camp or a mountain redoubt, but a walled town with administrative offices, granaries, and a tax register. The psychological and practical significance was enormous. The movement now had a territorial address.[^luo]

## The Yong'an siege (September 1851 – April 1852)

Saišangga's forces surrounded Yong'an within weeks of its fall. Qing troops constructed a siege ring — trenches, breastworks, artillery positions — on the high ground around the town. The encirclement was intended to starve the Taiping into submission over the winter. Qing dispatches to Beijing reported the siege as effective, but the reality was less certain: Qing commanders, anxious to minimize their own casualties and wary of Taiping sorties, maintained a loose blockade rather than a tight assault cordon.[^mao]

Conditions inside Yong'an were harsh but not yet desperate. The Taiping had brought grain reserves with them, and the town's own granaries supplemented their stores. But the civilian population — the town's original residents plus the thousands of Taiping camp followers — strained the food supply. The Taiping court used the siege period to impose order: sex segregation was enforced, the Sacred Treasury (圣库, Shèngkù) centralized goods, and discipline codes were applied with public executions. Yang Xiuqing, now undisputed as military and administrative chief under Hong Xiuquan, ran the city like a garrison under permanent siege.[^kuhn]

## The Yong'an court formation (永安建制)

The most enduring achievement of the Yong'an period was the formal establishment of the Taiping royal hierarchy, known in Chinese historiography as the Yong'an court formation (永安建制, Yǒng'ān jiànzhì). Hong Xiuquan, already the Heavenly King (天王, Tiānwáng), named five subordinate kings, each with a directional title and a defined position in the command structure:[^luo]

- **Yang Xiuqing** (杨秀清): Eastern King (东王, Dōngwáng), chief minister and commander of all subordinate kings, with authority over military and civil affairs second only to Hong. His claim to speak with the voice of God the Heavenly Father was now institutionalized as an organ of state.
- **Xiao Chaogui** (萧朝贵): Western King (西王, Xīwáng), second to Yang, commanding the forward units that would eventually lead the breakout into Hunan.
- **Feng Yunshan** (冯云山): Southern King (南王, Nánwáng), the movement's original organizer and Hong's earliest companion, responsible for administration, texts, and theology.
- **Wei Changhui** (韦昌辉): Northern King (北王, Běiwáng), a wealthy former landlord who had funded the movement's early weapons purchases, given authority over the treasury and urban security.
- **Shi Dakai** (石达开): Assistant King (翼王, Yìwáng), the youngest of the five, commanding mobile operations and external contacts.

The Yong'an period also saw the introduction of the Taiping calendar (太平天国历, Tàipíng Tiānguó Lì) — a 366-day solar calendar with twelve months of 30 or 31 days, no intercalary months, and a seven-day week with Saturday Sabbath. The calendar was part of a broader assertion of sacred time: the Taiping rejected the Qing calendar, with its lunar calculations and its designation of "auspicious" and "inauspicious" days, as a tool of imperial and geomantic superstition. In its place they imposed their own temporal order, dated from the Heavenly Kingdom's founding.[^michael1]

The Sacred Treasury system was formalized: all property belonged to the Heavenly Father, all goods flowed through the treasury, and all rations were issued by it. This was less an economic policy than a survival mechanism — a way to feed an army that could not buy food on the open market while surrounded by the enemy — but it embodied the communitarian ideology that would later be articulated in the Land System.[^kuhn]

## The breakout (5 April 1852)

By April 1852, with food exhausted and Saišangga's forces tightening the cordon, the Taiping leadership prepared a breakout. The operation was daring: the entire army — soldiers, porters, women, children, and the new court apparatus — would evacuate the town under cover of darkness and fight through Qing lines at a pre-selected point of weakness.

On the night of 5 April, the army abandoned Yong'an and struck Qing positions at Gushou (古苏). Luo Ergang describes the operation as the first major test of Taiping military discipline under siege conditions: the breakout required moving thousands of people through narrow mountain passes while fighting rearguard actions against pursuing Qing cavalry. The Taiping suffered heavy casualties — perhaps 2,000–3,000 dead or captured, including many of the original Guangxi veterans — but the bulk of the army escaped. The alternative was not escape or surrender, but annihilation.[^luo]

## The march north through Guangxi

From Yong'an the Taiping pushed north through Guangxi, fighting a series of running engagements. In April–May 1852 they attempted to besiege Guilin (桂林), the provincial capital. The siege lasted thirty-three days and failed. Guilin's defenses, strengthened by Qing reinforcements and the arrival of Jiang Zhongyuan (江忠源), a Hunan militia commander who would become one of the Taiping's most effective early foes, proved too strong. The Taiping abandoned the siege and moved on.

The army pressed toward the Hunan border. At Quanzhou (全州), a walled town in northeastern Guangxi, the Taiping fought what was in retrospect one of the most consequential engagements of the entire northern march. On or about 24 May 1852, during an assault on the town, Feng Yunshan — the Southern King, the movement's founder-organizer, the man who had built the God Worshipping Society from a handful of converts in Thistle Mountain — was struck by Qing cannon fire. He died of his wounds shortly afterward. The Taiping leadership had lost its most experienced organizer and the man closest to Hong Xiuquan since the 1840s.[^michael1][^luo]

Feng's death was a political disaster concealed by military momentum. The army continued north, crossed into Hunan, and in August 1852 reached the provincial capital of Changsha, where a second royal death — that of Xiao Chaogui on 12 September — would further reduce the inner circle. But the Guangxi campaigns were finished. The Taiping had left their native province behind, and they would never return.

## Debates

Historians have debated whether the Yong'an siege period was necessary or whether it was a strategic error that allowed Qing forces to organize while Taiping strength was at its greatest relative to government military capability. Michael notes that the Taiping court used Yong'an to formalize institutions — the kingship system, the calendar, the treasury — that defined the state's internal structure thereafter. The counterargument, associated with some Chinese military historians, holds that the Taiping should have broken out earlier and maintained momentum rather than allowing Saišangga to organize an encirclement. Luo Ergang takes a middle position: the Yong'an period was necessary for state-building but the breakout was delayed too long, and the casualties suffered during the April 1852 fighting weakened the army precisely when it needed maximum strength for the campaigns ahead.[^luo][^mao]

The relative weight of factors in Feng Yunshan's death also remains debated. Some scholars treat it as battlefield chance — an unlucky cannon hit — while others see it as evidence of the Taiping's tactical immaturity in siege operations: had proper assault trenches and artillery positions been prepared, Quanzhou might have been taken without exposing a senior commander to direct fire. The loss of Feng, combined with Xiao Chaogui's death at Changsha three months later, removed two of the five subordinate kings; the surviving three (Yang, Wei, and Shi) would become the unstable triumvirate that governed until the Tianjing Incident of 1856.[^luo]
