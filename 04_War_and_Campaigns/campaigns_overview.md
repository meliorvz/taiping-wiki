# Campaigns Overview

The Taiping war, fought between 1851 and 1864, unfolded across central and eastern China and can be divided into three broad phases. The first phase (1851–1853) was a mobile insurgency that carried the Taiping from a remote Guangxi village to the captured Ming-dynasty capital of Nanjing. The second phase (1853–1856) saw the regime attempt simultaneous expansion across multiple fronts while the Qing improvised a military response. The third phase (1856–1864) began with the internal catastrophe of the Tianjing Incident and ended with the systematic Qing reconquest of the Yangzi valley under provincial armies, local militia, and foreign-supported forces.[^michael-vol1]

## Phase 1: From Jintian to Nanjing (January 1851 – March 1853)

The Taiping movement began military operations at the Jintian uprising on 11 January 1851, when several thousand members of the God Worshipping Society (拜上帝会, Bài Shàngdì Huì) gathered at Jintian village in Guiping County, Guangxi, declared open rebellion, and repulsed an initial Qing attack. Hong Xiuquan took the title of Heavenly King (天王, Tiānwáng), and the core leadership designated themselves as kings of the four cardinal directions.[^luo]

After breaking through Qing encirclement at Jintian, the Taiping army seized the walled town of Yong'an (永安, Yǒng'ān) in September 1851, where they remained through the winter, established a rudimentary court structure, and formally conferred royal ranks on the five senior kings. In April 1852 they broke out of a Qing siege at Yong'an and began a fighting retreat northward through Guangxi. The march covered some 1,500 kilometers: they bypassed the provincial capital Guilin after a 33-day siege (April–May 1852), moved into Hunan, and besieged Changsha for 81 days (September–November 1852) — an operation in which the Southern King Feng Yunshan and the Western King Xiao Chaogui were both killed.[^michael-vol1]

From Changsha the Taiping crossed Dongting Lake, captured Yiyang and Yuezhou, and seized thousands of boats. They descended the Yangzi River in late 1852, took the triple city of Wuhan — Wuchang, Hanyang, and Hankou — in December 1852 to January 1853, acquiring enormous quantities of silver, grain, weapons, and recruits. Luo Ergang estimates that by the time they reached Wuhan the Taiping army numbered approximately 500,000–600,000 persons including camp followers and families, though the effective fighting force was approximately 300,000–500,000 combatants.[^luo] In February 1853 the army loaded its forces onto a vast flotilla and descended the Yangzi. Nanjing, the former Ming capital, fell on 19 March 1853 and was renamed Tianjing (天京, Tiānjīng, "Heavenly Capital").

## Phase 2: Expansion and Strain (1853–1856)

With Tianjing as their base, the Taiping leadership launched three major military initiatives. First, a Northern Expedition (北伐, Běifá) was dispatched in May 1853 under the command of Lin Fengxiang (林凤祥) and Li Kaifang (李开芳). This force of roughly 20,000–30,000 troops advanced northward from Pukou through Anhui, Henan, and into Zhili province, reaching as far as Jinghai, within approximately 160 kilometers of Beijing.[^kuhn] The expedition scattered Qing forces across the north and threw the court into alarm, but without reinforcement, supplies, or a secure base it could not sustain itself. A relief force commanded by Zeng Lichang was destroyed, and the original northern army was annihilated by the spring of 1855.[^michael-vol1]

Second, Western Expeditions (西征, Xīzhēng) moved up the Yangzi in 1853–1856 with the aim of securing the middle river valley, its grain-producing regions, and the strategic cities of Anqing, Jiujiang, and Wuchang. These campaigns produced the Taiping state's deepest territorial expansion but also provoked a counter-response that would ultimately determine the war's outcome. Hunan gentry under Zeng Guofan (曾国藩) raised the Xiang Army (湘军, Xiāngjūn), a new kind of provincial force recruited from rural bonds of kinship and locality, funded by local gentry, and motivated by Confucian loyalty.[^kuhn]

Third, a series of eastern campaigns sought to secure the lower Yangzi approaches to Tianjing and to break the Qing siege camps (江北、江南大营, Jiāngběi and Jiāngnán dàyíng) that the imperial government had established north and south of the capital. The Taiping scored significant victories, shattering the Jiangnan Great Camp in June 1856 — their high-water mark militarily. At this moment of apparent strength, the movement suffered its greatest internal crisis.

## Phase 3: Qing Recovery and Taiping Collapse (1856–1864)

The Tianjing Incident (天京事变, Tiānjīng shìbiàn) of September–November 1856 shattered the Taiping central leadership. The Eastern King Yang Xiuqing, who had accumulated near-absolute authority through his claimed divine revelations, was killed along with thousands of his followers by the Northern King Wei Changhui on Hong Xiuquan's orders. Wei was then executed for excessive violence. The Assistant King Shi Dakai, the movement's most capable field commander, fled Tianjing with a large contingent of troops and operated independently thereafter, eventually being destroyed in Sichuan in 1863. The incident removed the original leadership cohort and fatally damaged trust between the capital and field commanders.[^michael-vol1]

The post-1856 Taiping state survived thanks to a new generation of commanders, above all Chen Yucheng (the Ying King, 英王) and Li Xiucheng (the Loyal King, 忠王), who won notable victories including the destruction of a Qing army at Sanhe (三河) in Anhui in November 1858. But the strategic balance had shifted permanently. Zeng Guofan's Xiang Army now commanded the middle Yangzi; Li Hongzhang's Huai Army (淮军, Huáijūn) mobilized the Shanghai region; and foreign intervention — first under Frederick Townsend Ward's foreign-officered force, then under Charles George Gordon's Ever Victorious Army — locked the Taiping out of the critical treaty-port zone in the lower Yangzi.[^wilson][^lindley]

The fall of Anqing in September 1861 opened the middle Yangzi to Qing forces and exposed Tianjing from the west. Zeng Guofan's forces, his brother Zeng Guoquan's siege corps, and Li Hongzhang's troops in Jiangsu executed a coordinated encirclement that tightened through 1862–1864. Hong Xiuquan died in Tianjing on 1 June 1864. The city fell to Zeng Guoquan's forces on 19 July 1864. Sporadic Taiping resistance continued in the south under remaining commanders through 1865–1866, but the Heavenly Kingdom as a state had ceased to exist.[^luo]

The total death toll of the war remains disputed; credible estimates range from 20 to 30 million, making it one of the deadliest civil wars in human history.

## Debates

Scholars continue to debate when exactly the Taiping lost the war. Some identify the Northern Expedition's failure (1855) as the decisive strategic moment, arguing it cost the movement its only chance at rapid dynastic victory. Others point to the Tianjing Incident (1856) as the internal rupture from which the regime never recovered. Still others, following Kuhn, emphasize the structural shift toward provincial militarization that produced qualitatively superior Qing armies after 1860. Luo Ergang treats the failure to win Anhui — culminating in the Anqing debacle — as the definitive turning point. The debate reflects different judgments about whether the Taiping lost the war or the Qing won it.

A second debate concerns the role of foreign intervention: Wilson and other contemporary British observers credited Gordon's campaigns with deciding the outcome, while later scholarship tends to see foreign forces as important but secondary to the provincial Chinese armies of Zeng Guofan and Li Hongzhang.
