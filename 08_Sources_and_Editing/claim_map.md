## Claim ID: CLM-0018

- **Existing wiki pages affected**: Li Xiucheng; fall of Nanjing; Tianjing Incident; Taiping primary sources
- **Action**: Add as standard source-critical note on all pages citing the confession; flag the Luo Ergang critical edition as the authoritative version

## Claim ID: CLM-0023

- **Claim**: Meyer-Fong's "what remains" thesis argues that the Taiping war's primary legacy in Jiangnan was not political or military but cultural and demographic: the war reshaped how communities remembered themselves, who was commemorated as loyal and who was erased, and the physical and human landscape of the lower Yangzi. This perspective challenges both the PRC "peasant revolution" narrative and the traditional military-political historiography.
- **Supported by**: SRC-0005 (Meyer-Fong)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Evidence category**: Later historical argument (Meyer-Fong's central thesis)
- **Confidence**: High (as an interpretive framework; individual findings within the book have varying confidence levels)
- **Existing wiki pages affected**: Historiography; postwar Jiangnan; commemoration; memory and the war
- **Action**: Add as a major historiographical position; present alongside other interpretive frameworks

## Claim ID: CLM-0024

- **Claim**: Kuhn's "militarization of local society" thesis argues that the Taiping Rebellion permanently altered state-society relations in late imperial China. The Qing state's reliance on locally raised, gentry-led armies (Xiang Army, Huai Army) transferred military and fiscal power from the central bureaucracy to regional officials and local elites. This decentralisation created conditions that persisted through the fall of the dynasty and into the warlord period.
- **Supported by**: SRC-0003 (Kuhn)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Evidence category**: Later historical argument (Kuhn's central thesis)
- **Confidence**: High (as an interpretive framework; widely accepted with qualifications)
- **Existing wiki pages affected**: Militarization; Xiang Army; Huai Army; post-Taiping Qing state; warlordism
- **Action**: Add as a major historiographical position; note that scholars since Kuhn have qualified the linearity of the "militarization leads to warlordism" chain

## Claim ID: CLM-0025

- **Supported by**: SRC-0004 (Reilly)

- **Action**: Add as a major historiographical position; present alongside socioeconomic and military interpretations; note that the thesis has been debated

## Claim ID: CLM-0026

- **Claim**: The Taiping calendar was a deliberate break from both the Chinese lunar calendar and the Western Gregorian calendar. It was based on a 366-day year, abolished traditional festivals and the sexagenary cycle, and established Sunday (礼拜天) as the weekly day of worship. The calendar caused practical difficulties and alienated the rural population, who relied on the traditional calendar for agricultural timing and festival observance.
- **Supported by**: SRC-0004 (Reilly), SRC-0029 (Kilcourse), SRC-0002 (Michael — documents on the calendar)
- **Source type**: Taiping primary (calendar edicts); Western scholarship
- **Evidence category**: Taiping administrative practice; later historical argument
- **Confidence**: High (for the existence of the Taiping calendar and its features); moderate (for the claim about alienating the rural population — plausible but harder to prove)
- **Existing wiki pages affected**: Taiping calendar; religion; rural administration
- **Action**: Add as factual claim about calendar features; qualify the alienation claim with "may have contributed to" rather than "caused"
