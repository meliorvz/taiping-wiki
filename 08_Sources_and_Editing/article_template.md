# Title / 中文题名

## Lead

Write two to four paragraphs that tell the reader who, what, where, when, and why it matters.

## Terms

List only key Chinese terms, period-specific vocabulary, and concepts that need defining *before* the narrative. This section is a glossary for the page, not a dumping ground for every term used. Items belong here when:
- A term appears frequently throughout the page and the reader needs a definition upfront to follow the narrative (e.g. 天京, Tiānjīng, "Heavenly Capital")
- A concept has a specific Taiping meaning that differs from its general usage (e.g. 圣库, Shèngkù, "Sacred Treasury")
- A rank, title, or institution is introduced that needs explanation before it appears in context (e.g. 东王, Dōngwáng, "Eastern King")

Terms that are explained on first use in the body text do not need to be repeated here. Use the standard format: Chinese characters, pinyin, English translation (e.g. 太平天国, Tàipíng Tiānguó, "Heavenly Kingdom of Great Peace").

## Narrative

Tell the story in chronological order unless the page is thematic.

## Consequences

Explain what changed because of the event, person, text, or institution.

## Debates

State the historiographical question clearly, present competing scholarly views with named scholars, and give the wiki's current working view. Structure each debate as:
1. **The question**: One sentence stating what scholars disagree about.
2. **Position A**: Named scholar(s) and their argument, with source.
3. **Position B**: Named scholar(s) and their counterargument, with source.
4. **Current working view**: The wiki's provisional conclusion, acknowledging uncertainty where it exists.

Use plain labels for arguments. Do not overfill with academic caution, but do name the scholars and their works. Only include debates that have a genuine scholarly literature behind them — do not invent controversies.

Example:
- **The question**: Was the Tianjing Incident primarily Hong's responsibility or Yang's?
- **Position A**: Luo Ergang assigns primary blame to Yang Xiuqing's ambition, arguing Yang violated the founding compact by demanding co-sovereignty.[^luo]
- **Position B**: Michael distributes blame more evenly, noting that Hong authorized a premeditated execution rather than attempting arbitration.[^michael]
- **Current working view**: The Taiping's institutional structure — fusing charisma, military command, and religious authority — made peaceful resolution impossible once two sacred voices clashed.
