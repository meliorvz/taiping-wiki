## Policies

### Land policy

- The 天朝田亩制度 (1853) proposed dividing all land into nine grades and distributing it according to household size, adjusted for age and gender. Land was state-owned in principle; private ownership was not recognised (SRC-0013, SRC-0002 Michael).
- The Land System was never fully implemented. In practice, the Taiping often maintained existing landholding patterns and collected taxes from landowners where possible, reverting to a modified Qing land-tax system (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).
- In some areas, the Taiping issued "land tickets" (田凭) recognising tenant cultivation rights; in others, they simply collected grain levies (SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).
- The system of 25-household units with shared granaries echoes the ancient well-field (井田) ideal and was likely aspirational (SRC-0002 Michael, SRC-0004 Reilly).

### Taxation

- The Taiping replaced the complex Qing tax structure with a simplified grain levy (漕粮 / 地丁) in areas they controlled. Rates varied by region and period (SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).
- Commercial taxes (厘金 / lijin) were introduced by the Qing side (specifically, by Lei Yixian in Jiangsu in 1853) as an innovative transit tax to fund military operations. This became a permanent feature of late Qing finance and a major source of revenue for the Xiang and Huai Armies (SRC-0003 Kuhn).
- The Taiping also levied commercial taxes in some areas, though the evidence is sparser (SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).

### Gender

- The Taiping proclaimed gender equality in religious terms (all people are children of God) and established separate women's military and administrative units (SRC-0004 Reilly).
- Women's Barracks (女館) in Nanjing housed tens of thousands of women in sex-segregated quarters. Marriage was prohibited in the early Nanjing period (1853-1855), then permitted after 1855 following leadership pressure (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).
- The Taiping prohibited foot-binding, though enforcement was uneven. Women served in labour battalions, fought in some military units, and sat for women's civil service examinations (SRC-0004 Reilly).
- In practice, women's status was highly circumscribed. The leadership (especially Hong Xiuquan) had multiple wives and concubines. Yang Xiuqing and others wielded power while maintaining patriarchal norms (SRC-0027 Spence, SRC-0005 Meyer-Fong).
- Meyer-Fong (SRC-0005) documents that women suffered disproportionately during the war: mass suicide to avoid capture, sexual violence, and postwar marginalisation in commemoration.

### Law and justice

- Taiping law was based on the Ten Commandments adapted to the Chinese context, supplemented by edicts from the Heavenly King and the Eastern King. Punishments were severe; adultery, theft, and opium use were capital crimes (SRC-0002 Michael, SRC-0004 Reilly).
- A reformed legal code was published (天條書, Book of Heavenly Commandments), circulated as a moral-legal guide for soldiers and civilians (SRC-0002 Michael).

### Religion

- The Taiping enforced exclusive worship of Shangdi (上帝), prohibiting ancestor worship, Buddhist and Daoist temple cults, geomancy (风水), and divination. This anti-idolatry campaign was among the most socially disruptive policies (SRC-0004 Reilly).
- Sunday (礼拜天) was observed as the Sabbath with communal worship services. The Taiping created their own calendar, which diverged from both the Chinese lunar calendar and the Western Gregorian calendar (SRC-0004 Reilly, SRC-0029 Kilcourse).
- Christian baptism (immersion) was practiced as a rite of entry into the community (SRC-0004 Reilly, SRC-0029 Kilcourse).
- The Taiping printed and distributed their own editions of the Bible (with annotations by Hong Xiuquan) and other religious texts (SRC-0004 Reilly).
- Missionaries who visited Nanjing (Issachar Roberts, Griffith John, Joseph Edkins, and others) were initially hopeful but ultimately disillusioned by what they saw as the Taiping's heretical version of Christianity (SRC-0016 British Parliamentary Papers, SRC-0004 Reilly).

## Campaign details

### Northern Expedition (1853-1855)

- Launched from Yangzhou in May 1853 with approximately 20,000-30,000 troops under Lin Fengxiang (林凤祥), Li Kaifang (李开芳), and Ji Wenyuan (吉文元) (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Advanced rapidly through Anhui, Henan, and into Zhili (modern Hebei), reaching within 100 km of Beijing (to Jinghai and Dugu, south of Tianjin) by October 1853 (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Stalled by winter, shortages, and Qing countermeasures. Reinforcements were blocked. The Southern branch of the Northern Expedition under Zeng Lichang was destroyed in Shandong (1854). The main force under Lin Fengxiang was besieged at Lianzhen (連鎮) and annihilated in February 1855. Li Kaifang's remnant at Fengguantun (馮官屯), Shandong, was destroyed in May 1855 (SRC-0001 Michael).
- The expedition was a major strategic error: it dispersed Taiping strength without capturing Beijing, and the elite troops lost were irreplaceable.

### Western Campaigns (1853-1856)

- Aimed at securing the middle Yangzi valley and the supply routes to the Heavenly Capital. Under Shi Dakai, Wei Changhui, and others (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Major gains included: retaking Jiujiang (1854-1855), capturing Wuchang for the second time (1855), and devastating the Xiang Army's early forces under Zeng Guofan (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Shi Dakai's victory at the Battle of the Xiang River (湘江之战, 1854-1855) nearly destroyed the Xiang Army and drove Zeng Guofan to attempt suicide twice (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0003 Kuhn).
- The campaign was interrupted by the Tianjing Incident (1856), which recalled key commanders to Nanjing and halted offensive momentum.

### Eastern Expedition (1860-1862)

- Under Li Xiucheng, the Taiping swept through southern Jiangsu in May-June 1860, capturing Suzhou, Changzhou, Wuxi, and other cities (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0011 Li Xiucheng).
- The advance on Shanghai was repulsed in August 1860 by Ward's forces and in 1862 by combined foreign and Qing forces (SRC-0010 Wilson).
- The campaign extended Taiping territory and provided grain and revenue for the Heavenly Capital but overextended Taiping forces at a critical moment when Anqing was under siege.

### Siege of Anqing (1860-1861)

- Zeng Guofan and his brother Zeng Guoquan besieged Anqing, the key Yangzi fortress in Anhui, from 1860 to September 1861 (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Multiple Taiping relief attempts under Chen Yucheng failed. Anqing fell in September 1861, cutting the Taiping's principal river supply route and exposing Nanjing to the Xiang Army's advance (SRC-0001 Michael).
- The loss of Anqing is widely regarded as the turning point from which the Taiping could not recover (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0003 Kuhn).

### Fall of Nanjing (1864)

- Zeng Guofan's forces besieged Nanjing from 1862. A tight encirclement was achieved by 1863. Hong Xiuquan refused repeated suggestions to abandon the city (SRC-0011 Li Xiucheng).
- Hong Xiuquan died on 1 June 1864. The city fell to a Xiang Army assault on 19 July 1864. Some sources say ~100,000 Taiping defenders and civilians were killed in the aftermath (SRC-0001 Michael). Zeng Guofan's own report claims 100,000 killed; other estimates are lower. Meyer-Fong (SRC-0005) emphasises the civilian catastrophe of the fall and the subsequent looting.
- Li Xiucheng escaped with the young Heavenly King (Hong Tianguifu) but was captured a few days later (SRC-0011).

## Casualty evidence

- The broad estimate of 20-30 million deaths is widely cited (SRC-0023 Britannica, SRC-0001 Michael, summary in SRC-0005 Meyer-Fong). This figure includes deaths from combat, starvation, disease, and massacre over the 1850-1864 period. The methodology behind this number is poorly documented.
- Meyer-Fong (SRC-0005) documents that some Jiangnan counties lost 50-70% of their registered population between pre-war and post-war censuses. The 太平天国资料 (SRC-0019) and provincial gazetteers (SRC-0021, SRC-0022) contain county-level population figures.
- The Tianjing Incident (1856): Wei Changhui's killings of Yang Xiuqing's followers are estimated at 20,000-40,000 (SRC-0001 Michael; SRC-0011 Li Xiucheng gives higher figures). The subsequent execution of Wei Changhui added several thousand more.
- The Suzhou Massacre (December 1863): Li Hongzhang ordered the execution of the surrendered Taiping garrison; estimates range from several thousand to 20,000 (SRC-0010 Wilson, SRC-0001 Michael).
- The fall of Nanjing (July 1864): Total deaths in the assault and aftermath are disputed. Zeng Guofan's official report claimed 100,000 Taiping killed; this may be inflated (SRC-0001 Michael).
- Lindley (SRC-0009) describes Qing massacres of civilians in graphic terms; his estimates should be treated as high-end and partisan.
- Disease (cholera, dysentery, smallpox) was a major killer on both sides, particularly in crowded siege conditions. No reliable aggregate figures exist.

## Local social evidence

- Pre-war God Worshipping Society drew primarily from Hakka (客家) communities in Guangxi, including charcoal burners, miners, and marginalised cultivators. The ethnic dimension (Hakka vs. Punti/bendi) was important in Guangxi recruitment but declined as the movement moved northward (SRC-0001 Michael, SRC-0004 Reilly).
- Women played key roles in the early Guangxi community, including as spirit mediums. The female leadership (Hong Xuanjiao, Su Sanniang, and others) is attested but poorly documented (SRC-0004 Reilly, SRC-0006 Luo Ergang).
- The war caused massive population displacement. Meyer-Fong (SRC-0005) describes refugee flows from Jiangnan to Shanghai, from Anhui to Hubei, and from Zhejiang to Fujian.
- Gazeteers (SRC-0021, SRC-0022) record the destruction of schools, examination halls, temples, bridges, and irrigation works. The physical infrastructure of local society was systematically devastated in contested areas.
- Postwar gazetteer entries systematically commemorate Qing loyalists while erasing or demonising those who served the Taiping. Meyer-Fong (SRC-0005) argues this produced a long-term memory distortion in favour of the Qing narrative.
- Local elites who survived the war often used militia organisation experience to expand their power in the postwar period. This is Kuhn's "militarization of local society" thesis (SRC-0003).

## Historiographical interpretations

| Interpretation | Proponent(s) | Key source | Summary |
|---------------|-------------|------------|---------|
| Peasant revolution | Luo Ergang, PRC orthodoxy | SRC-0006 | Taiping was a class-based peasant uprising against feudal exploitation. The movement's progressive elements (land redistribution, gender equality) are foregrounded; its religious character is treated as secondary. |
| Militarization of local society | Philip A. Kuhn | SRC-0003 | The Taiping Rebellion permanently altered state-society relations by forcing the Qing to rely on locally raised armies. This militarization persisted into the 20th century and was a key antecedent of warlordism. |
| Blasphemy of empire | Thomas H. Reilly | SRC-0004 | The Taiping's religious character was not incidental but central. Their comprehensive attack on Qing symbolic and ritual order — ancestor worship, Confucian examinations, temple cults — constituted a "blasphemy of empire" that was more socially disruptive than their military campaign. |
| What remains: the civilian experience | Tobie Meyer-Fong | SRC-0005 | Shifts the focus from politics and battles to the human experience of civil war — mass death, displacement, trauma, and selective postwar commemoration. Challenges the state-centred narratives of both Qing and PRC historiography. |
| Localization of Christianity | Carl Kilcourse | SRC-0029 | Taiping religion was a coherent "localization" of Christianity to the Chinese context, not a garbled misunderstanding. The Taiping engaged seriously with Christian theology while adapting it to Chinese cultural categories. |
| Internal collapse thesis | Michael, Luo Ergang | SRC-0001, SRC-0006 | The Tianjing Incident of 1856 and subsequent leadership fractures were the primary cause of Taiping decline. External military pressure was less important than internal political failure. |
| Hakka identity thesis | Various (partially supported by Spence, SRC-0027) | Multiple | The Taiping drew strength from Hakka ethnic solidarity and the social marginality of Hakka communities in Guangxi. This ethnic dimension declined as the movement moved northward but remained important in leadership cohesion. |
| Foreign intervention was secondary | Michael, Kuhn, Luo Ergang | SRC-0001, SRC-0003, SRC-0006 | The Ever-Victorious Army and British/French intervention were tactically significant in the lower Yangzi but not decisive to the overall outcome. The Xiang and Huai armies won the war. |
| Selective postwar memory | Meyer-Fong | SRC-0005 | Postwar gazetteers and shrines systematically privileged Qing loyalist perspectives and erased the Taiping experience. This produced a skewed historical record that still shapes how the war is understood. |
| PRC historiographical evolution | Liu Chen, recent Chinese scholarship | SRC-0008 | Chinese scholarship has moved significantly away from the Mao-era "peasant revolution" framework since the 1990s, incorporating religious, cultural, and social history approaches. |
| Liang Fa thesis | Reilly, Spence | SRC-0004, SRC-0027 | Hong Xiuquan's Christian understanding was mediated primarily through Liang Fa's 劝世良言 (Good Words to Admonish the Age), a Chinese Protestant tract that shaped his particular interpretation of Christianity. |
| Chinese sectarian roots | Reilly, Kilcourse | SRC-0004, SRC-0029 | Taiping religion drew not only on Protestant Christianity but also on indigenous Chinese sectarian traditions (especially millenarian and syncretic movements), which provided models for trance mediumship, communal organisation, and apocalyptic expectation. |
| Qing state transformation thesis | Kuhn | SRC-0003 | The Taiping Rebellion was the catalyst that transformed the Qing state from a centralised bureaucratic empire to a system dependent on regional power-holders (governors-general with military and fiscal autonomy). This decentralisation persisted through the dynasty's end. |
| Mid-Qing crisis thesis | Kuhn, broader scholarship | SRC-0003 | The Taiping Rebellion was the most severe manifestation of a broader mid-19th century crisis: population pressure, ecological strain, fiscal exhaustion, administrative corruption, and sectarian unrest. The White Lotus Rebellion (1796-1804) and the Nian Rebellion were related phenomena. |
