## Source ID: SRC-0001

- **Title**: The Taiping Rebellion: History and Documents, Vol. I (History)
- **Chinese title**: N/A
- **English translation**: N/A
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Franz H. Michael (in collaboration with Chung-li Chang)
- **Date of composition**: 1966
- **Publication or edition**: University of Washington Press, Seattle; Far Eastern and Russian Institute Publications on Asia No. 17
- **Genre**: Western academic survey with documentary introduction
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/english_scholarship/Michael_Vol1_History_1966.pdf` (15 MB, 264 pp, LibGen MD5 `3e2f66e0dbb9463a46a5dc091d77f7be`)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Reliability tier**: 2
- **Known bias**: Divides Taiping history into phases using Western periodisation conventions; subordinates Chinese institutional analysis to narrative military-political history.
- **Main contribution**: The first comprehensive English-language narrative of the entire rebellion. Organises events into six phases (opium war to Jintian, Jintian to Nanjing, Northern Expedition, internal strife and decline, foreign intervention, final collapse). Provides the periodisation framework still used in most Western textbooks.
- **Known problems**: Relies heavily on Qing sources translated by the editors, which embed Qing biases. Does not use gazetteer evidence. Underplays the role of militia and local society, which Kuhn would later explore.
- **Related wiki pages**: Background (Opium War, early missionary contacts); Jintian Uprising; capture of Nanjing; Northern Expedition; Tianjing Incident; fall of the Heavenly Kingdom
- **Usable excerpts**: Michael's six-phase periodisation; the translated imperial edict of January 1851 declaring Hong Xiuquan an outlaw; summary of British diplomatic assessments in 1853-54.
- **Claims supported**: That the Taiping was the largest civil war of the 19th century; that it drew strength from anti-Manchu sentiment; that leadership infighting was the proximate cause of decline; that foreign intervention was secondary to internal causes of collapse.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping had a realistic chance of toppling the Qing after 1856; that foreign powers were ever close to recognising the Taiping regime.
- **Recommended citation form**: Franz H. Michael, *The Taiping Rebellion: History and Documents*, Vol. I: History (Seattle: University of Washington Press, 1966).

- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship (documentary translation)
- **Reliability tier**: 2
- **Known bias**: Selection of ~400 documents favours those in U.S., British, and French archives; some Qing military documents translated from earlier published collections rather than original archives. Comments reflect 1960s-70s Western academic consensus.
- **Main contribution**: The largest single collection of Taiping and Qing documents in English translation. - **Known problems**: Some translations have been superseded by more recent scholarship (eg. Reilly 2004 on theological terminology). The commentary becomes dated in places on the role of religion. The Western missionary documents are selectively deployed.
- **Related wiki pages**: Land System; Hong Rengan and the New Deal; Taiping theology; Taiping printing; British policy toward the Taiping
- **Usable excerpts**: Full English text of the Land System decree; Hong Rengan's 1859 reform proposal; Taiping army regulations; correspondence between British envoys and Taiping officials in Nanjing.
- **Claims supported**: That the Land System decree was an ideal program, not implementable administrative policy; that Hong Rengan's reforms were blocked by existing Taiping leadership; that British neutrality hardened after 1856.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping produced no coherent administrative documents; that Western powers had uniform views of the Taiping.
- **Recommended citation form**: Franz H. Michael, ed., *The Taiping Rebellion: History and Documents*, Vols. II-III: Documents and Comments (Seattle: University of Washington Press, 1971).

## Source ID: SRC-0003

- **Title**: Rebellion and Its Enemies in Late Imperial China: Militarization and Social Structure, 1796-1864
- **Chinese title**: N/A
- **English translation**: N/A
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Philip A. Kuhn
- **Date of composition**: 1970
- **Publication or edition**: Harvard University Press, Cambridge, MA; Harvard East Asian Series 49
- **Genre**: Academic monograph (social and military history)
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/english_scholarship/Kuhn_Rebellion_and_Its_Enemies_1970.pdf` (57 MB, 270 pp, LibGen MD5 `d8f71918b56668791c063cd658b21adb`)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Reliability tier**: 1
- **Known bias**: Focuses on the Qing response rather than the Taiping itself; the "militarization" thesis foregrounds structural over contingent factors; draws mostly on Hunan, Hubei, and Anhui evidence.
- **Main contribution**: Established the thesis that the Taiping Rebellion permanently altered the relationship between local society and the imperial state by fostering large-scale militarization at the county and sub-county level. Traces the shift from orthodox baojia to military tuanlian (militia) to the regional armies (Xiang Army, Huai Army) under Han Chinese officials. Argues this created a "militarized class" that persisted into the 20th century.
- **Known problems**: Underplays the Ming loyalist and anti-Manchu traditions that gave the Taiping ideological appeal beyond mere banditry. The "militarization" model works less well for provinces where locally raised armies were less important (Jiangnan). Does not address the Taiping side's own military organization in comparable depth.
- **Related wiki pages**: Militia system; Xiang Army; Huai Army; Zeng Guofan; post-Taiping reconstruction; gentry power
- **Usable excerpts**: Analysis of baojia vs. tuanlian distinction; description of Zeng Guofan's recruitment methods for the Xiang Army; description of the "gentry manager" role in local defence.
- **Claims supported**: That the Qing regular forces (Green Standard, Banner) proved incapable of defeating the Taiping; that the Xiang Army's success depended on personal loyalty bonds, higher pay, and local recruitment; that post-rebellion the local gentry held unprecedented military-administrative power.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Qing suppression was a simple military victory; that the rebellion left the state structure unchanged.
- **Recommended citation form**: Philip A. Kuhn, *Rebellion and Its Enemies in Late Imperial China: Militarization and Social Structure, 1796-1864* (Cambridge, MA: Harvard University Press, 1970).

## Source ID: SRC-0004

- **Title**: The Taiping Heavenly Kingdom: Rebellion and the Blasphemy of Empire
- **Chinese title**: N/A
- **English translation**: N/A
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Thomas H. Reilly
- **Date of composition**: 2004
- **Publication or edition**: University of Washington Press, Seattle; China Program Book series
- **Genre**: Academic monograph (religious and intellectual history)
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/english_scholarship/Reilly_Taiping_Heavenly_Kingdom_2004.pdf` (1 MB, 248 pp, LibGen MD5 `bed361990c0898b3b9a4f2d71af625f3`)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Reliability tier**: 2
- **Known bias**: Foregrounds the religious dimension against the dominant socioeconomic interpretation; argues that previous scholarship underestimated the systematic coherence of Taiping theology. Sympathetic to the Taiping religious project without endorsing it.
- **Main contribution**: Argues that the Taiping represented a radical "blasphemy of empire" — a comprehensive attack on the symbolic and ritual foundations of Qing rule, not merely a military rising. Analyses how Taiping Christianity drew on Protestant missionary texts, Liang Fa's 劝世良言 (Good Words to Admonish the Age), and indigenous Chinese sectarian traditions. Contends that Taiping anti-idolatry was the movement's most socially disruptive policy because it targeted ancestor worship, temple cults, and the Confucian examination system simultaneously.
- **Known problems**: Focuses heavily on the early religious and ideological dimensions; less detailed on military or administrative history. The "blasphemy" thesis may overstate the coherence of Taiping ideological intentions.
- **Related wiki pages**: Taiping Christianity; Hong Xiuquan's visions; Liang Fa and Good Words; iconoclasm; examination system abolition
- **Usable excerpts**: Analysis of Taiping anti-idolatry campaigns; discussion of how the Taiping adapted Trinitarian theology with Hong Xiuquan as younger brother of Jesus; treatment of the Sabbath and Ten Commandments in Taiping practice.
- **Claims supported**: That Taiping religious ideology was internally coherent and not merely a garbled version of Christianity; that the attack on ancestor worship was a deliberate challenge to the Confucian family order; that the Taiping drew on Chinese sectarian as well as missionary sources.
- **Claims contradicted**: That Taiping Christianity was simply misunderstood Protestantism; that the religious dimension was epiphenomenal to class conflict.
- **Recommended citation form**: Thomas H. Reilly, *The Taiping Heavenly Kingdom: Rebellion and the Blasphemy of Empire* (Seattle: University of Washington Press, 2004).

## Source ID: SRC-0005

- **Title**: What Remains: Coming to Terms with Civil War in 19th Century China
- **Chinese title**: N/A
- **English translation**: N/A
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Tobie Meyer-Fong
- **Date of composition**: 2013
- **Publication or edition**: Stanford University Press, Stanford, CA
- **Genre**: Academic monograph (cultural and social history; memory studies)
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/english_scholarship/Meyer-Fong_What_Remains_2013.pdf` (27 MB, 335 pp, LibGen MD5 `fc0115e90a21bf3ff6acd8253bfb241d`)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Western scholarship
- **Reliability tier**: 1
- **Known bias**: Focuses on the Jiangnan region (Jiangsu-Zhejiang) and the postwar civilian experience, rather than the military-political history. Explicitly foregrounds the perspectives of the dead, displaced, and traumatised.
- **Main contribution**: Shifts the historiography from the politics of the Taiping to the human experience of civil war. Analyses how communities coped with mass death, physical destruction, population displacement, and the collapse of moral and social order. Uses gazetteers, literati diaries, memorial inscriptions, and local records to reconstruct the texture of postwar life. Argues that the physical and psychological devastation of the war reshaped Chinese culture and memory in ways that persisted into the 20th century.
- **Known problems**: Limited to Jiangnan regional evidence; does not address western China, north China, or the mid-Yangzi equally. The emphasis on memory and commemoration sometimes underweights economic and demographic recovery data.
- **Related wiki pages**: Death toll; refugee experience; postwar Jiangnan; gender and war; gazetteer commemoration; shrines for the war dead
- **Usable excerpts**: Descriptions of postwar corpse collection and mass burial; accounts of women who committed suicide to avoid capture; gazetteer records of local shrines to the loyal dead; the "number one calamity" trope in postwar local histories.
- **Claims supported**: That the war caused demographic catastrophe in Jiangnan, with some counties losing 50-70% of population; that postwar commemoration was controlled by Qing loyalist elites who shaped the memory of the war; that women's experiences were systematically underrepresented in official records.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping conflict was primarily a contest between two organised states, rather than a devastating social breakdown for civilians.
- **Recommended citation form**: Tobie Meyer-Fong, *What Remains: Coming to Terms with Civil War in 19th Century China* (Stanford: Stanford University Press, 2013).

## Source ID: SRC-0006

- **Title**: 太平天国史 (全4册) / History of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom (4 vols)
- **Chinese title**: 太平天国史
- **English translation**: History of the Taiping Heavenly Kingdom
- **Author/editor/compiler**: 罗尔纲 (Luo Ergang)
- **Date of composition**: 1991
- **Publication or edition**: 中华书局 (Zhonghua Book Company), Beijing; ISBN 7101003931
- **Genre**: PRC academic comprehensive history (88 juan)
- **Language**: Chinese
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/chinese_scholarship/Luo_Ergang_Taiping_Tianguo_Shi_Vol1.pdf`, Vol2.pdf, Vol3.pdf, Vol4.pdf (~100 MB total, 88 juan, via GitHub/Z-Library)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDFs acquired; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: PRC scholarship
- **Reliability tier**: 1
- **Known bias**: Luo Ergang is the foundational figure of modern Taiping studies in China. His work reflects the PRC orthodox interpretation of the Taiping as a "peasant revolutionary movement" (农民革命运动). The 1991 edition partially revises earlier Mao-era formulations but retains the revolutionary framework. Sympathetic to the Taiping.
- **Main contribution**: The single most comprehensive treatment of the Taiping in any language. 88 juan cover political history, institutions, military campaigns, biographies, economic policies, cultural policies, and foreign relations. Incorporates Luo's lifetime of archival work, including critical editions of key Taiping documents. The biographical sections are particularly valuable for minor and mid-level figures.
- **Known problems**: Dates the movement's origin to 1843 Hong Xiuquan's visions rather than 1851 Jintian, reflecting a PRC teleology. Suppresses or minimizes evidence of Taiping brutality. The "peasant revolution" framing obscures the religious and sectarian dimensions. Some document transcriptions have been corrected by later editors.
- **Related wiki pages**: All main pages on Taiping history; biographies of major figures; institutions; land policy; religion
- **Usable excerpts**: Biographical entries for the Six Kings; analysis of the 天朝田亩制度; detailed campaign itineraries; administrative structure of the Heavenly Capital.
- **Claims supported**: That the Taiping established a functioning (if uneven) administration over large territories; that land policy was announced but variably implemented; that the movement had coherent military organization through the "military-civilian" system (军民政合一).
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping was simply a destructive bandit movement without constructive programs.
- **Recommended citation form**: 罗尔纲, 《太平天国史》 (全4册), 北京: 中华书局, 1991年.

- **Chinese title**: N/A (Lindley's Chinese name: 林利, Lin-Le)
- **English translation**: N/A (original English)
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Augustus F. Lindley (Lin-Le)
- **Date of composition**: 1866
- **Publication or edition**: Day & Son, London
- **Genre**: Contemporary pro-Taiping memoir/military account
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/contemporary_accounts/Lindley_Vol1_1866.pdf` (516 pp), `Vol2_1866.pdf` (466 pp); also Project Gutenberg ebooks #39180, #39735
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDFs acquired via Internet Archive; full Gutenberg plain-text versions available)
- **Source type**: Foreign (pro-Taiping) contemporary account
- **Reliability tier**: 4 (heavily partisan)
- **Known bias**: Lindley served as a British officer in the Taiping forces under Li Xiucheng. He is an unabashed partisan who portrays the Taiping as Christian reformers oppressed by a tyrannical Qing. He exaggerates Taiping military successes and minimises Taiping atrocities. - **Main contribution**: The only book-length first-hand account by a Westerner who served inside the Taiping military. Provides unique details about Taiping military organisation, the personalities of leaders (especially Li Xiucheng), life in Taiping-controlled territories, and the nature of the Qing counterinsurgency. His illustrations and maps are rare visual sources.
- **Known problems**: Lindley's reliability on any matter favourable to the Taiping is suspect. He was writing to advocate for British recognition of the Taiping regime. Dates and troop numbers are frequently inflated. His descriptions of Qing atrocities are corroborated by other sources but may be selectively deployed.
- **Related wiki pages**: Li Xiucheng; foreign volunteers in the Taiping; Taiping military organisation; British policy
- **Usable excerpts**: Descriptions of the Taiping camp at Nanjing; accounts of Li Xiucheng's appearance and manner; details of Taiping military equipment and provisioning; his map of the Nanjing region.
- **Claims supported**: That foreign officers served in Taiping forces; that Li Xiucheng was a capable and humane commander; that the Qing forces committed widespread atrocities against civilians.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping were purely destructive bandits; that the Qing were the legitimate government of all Chinese.
- **Recommended citation form**: Augustus F. Lindley (Lin-Le), *Ti-ping Tien-kwoh: The History of the Ti-ping Revolution*, 2 vols. (London: Day & Son, 1866).

- **English translation**: N/A (original English)
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Andrew Wilson
- **Date of composition**: 1868
- **Publication or edition**: William Blackwood and Sons, Edinburgh and London
- **Genre**: Contemporary military history/journalism
- **Language**: English
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/contemporary_accounts/Wilson_Ever_Victorious_Army_1868.pdf` (444 pp, multiple IA scans)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired via Internet Archive; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Foreign (pro-Qing) contemporary account
- **Reliability tier**: 3
- **Known bias**: Wilson was a journalist favouring the British imperial presence in China. He portrays Gordon as a hero and the Ever-Victorious Army as a disciplined modern force. Qing forces are depicted as chaotic auxiliaries. Taiping are presented as fanatical rebels.
- **Main contribution**: The most detailed contemporary account of the Ever-Victorious Army (常胜军), the Western-officered mercenary force that fought alongside the Qing from 1860-1864. - **Known problems**: Overstates the Ever-Victorious Army's contribution to the Qing victory. Understates the role of Li Hongzhang's Huai Army and Zeng Guofan's Xiang Army. The Suzhou Massacre (December 1863) is handled defensively. Chinese names are romanised inconsistently.
- **Related wiki pages**: Ever-Victorious Army; Charles Gordon; Frederick Townsend Ward; Li Hongzhang; Huai Army; Suzhou Massacre
- **Usable excerpts**: Detailed descriptions of EVA operations around Shanghai and Suzhou; accounts of Gordon's tactical methods; description of the EVA's use of steamships on the Yangzi waterways.
- **Claims supported**: That a small, well-armed foreign-led force could be tactically decisive in the lower Yangzi theatre; that the EVA used superior firepower (artillery, repeating rifles) against massed Taiping formations.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the EVA was the primary agent of the Qing victory (rather than Li Hongzhang's Huai Army).
- **Recommended citation form**: Andrew Wilson, *The Ever-Victorious Army: A History of the Chinese Campaign under Lt. Col. C. G. Gordon* (Edinburgh: Blackwood, 1868).

- **Title**: 增补本李秀成自述原稿注 / Annotated Critical Edition of Li Xiucheng's Confession (Revised and Enlarged)
- **Chinese title**: 增补本李秀成自述原稿注
- **English translation**: Annotated Critical Edition of Li Xiucheng's Confession
- **Author/editor/compiler**: 罗尔纲 (Luo Ergang), editor and annotator. Original text by 李秀成 (Li Xiucheng).
- **Date of composition**: 1995 (critical edition); original confession written 1864
- **Publication or edition**: 中国社会科学出版社 (China Social Sciences Press), Beijing
- **Genre**: Annotated critical edition of a prisoner confession
- **Language**: Chinese (original and annotation)
- **Repository or file**: LibGen ID 3006687; 17 MB PDF
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired via LibGen; full text extraction pending)
- **Source type**: Taiping primary (confession) + PRC scholarship (annotation)
- **Reliability tier**: 2 for the text; 1 for Luo Ergang's annotation
- **Known bias**: Li Xiucheng wrote under duress in Zeng Guofan's custody after the fall of Nanjing, expecting execution. His text is partly a plea for mercy, partly a defence of his record, and partly an internal history of the Taiping. Zeng Guofan altered the text before submitting it to the Qing court; scholars debate how much was excised or modified. Luo Ergang's 1995 critical edition is the most authoritative version, collating multiple manuscript traditions and identifying Zeng's edits.
- **Main contribution**: The longest and most detailed first-hand Taiping account of the rebellion's internal history. Covers the Jintian Uprising, the early campaigns, the Tianjing Incident, Li's own military career (especially the 1860-1863 campaigns), and the final defence of Nanjing. Provides invaluable information on Taiping command structure, logistics, and factional politics.
- **Known problems**: Li Xiucheng was incentivised to minimise his own culpability and shift blame to Hong Xiuquan's decisions. The text was subject to Qing censorship by Zeng Guofan. Some scholars (notably Luo Ergang) argue the original manuscript was cut and rearranged. The 1864 political context shaped every statement.
- **Related wiki pages**: Li Xiucheng; fall of Nanjing; Tianjing Incident; Hong Xiuquan's last years; Taiping military organisation
- **Usable excerpts**: Li Xiucheng's account of the Tianjing Incident; his descriptions of the 1860 Eastern Expedition and capture of Suzhou; his assessment of foreign intervention; his account of Hong Xiuquan's refusal to abandon Nanjing.
- **Claims supported**: That Yang Xiuqing's ambition to supplant Hong Xiuquan triggered the Tianjing Incident; that Hong Xiuquan became increasingly detached and incompetent in the final years; that Li Xiucheng was the kingdom's most capable late-period commander.
- **Claims contradicted**: That Hong Xiuquan was a blameless victim of usurpers; that the Taiping collapse was due solely to external military pressure.
- **Recommended citation form**: 罗尔纲 (注), 《增补本李秀成自述原稿注》, 北京: 中国社会科学出版社, 1995年.

## Source ID: SRC-0012

- **Title**: 英杰归真 / Ying jie gui zhen / Heroes Return to the Truth
- **Chinese title**: 英杰归真
- **English translation**: Heroes Return to the Truth
- **Author/editor/compiler**: 何春发 (He Chunfa), under the direction of Hong Rengan
- **Date of composition**: 1861 (Taiping xinwei year 11)
- **Publication or edition**: Taiping court publication, Nanjing; Library of Congress digital record https://www.loc.gov/item/2021666321/
- **Genre**: Taiping religious-political pamphlet
- **Language**: Chinese (classical/vernacular mix, Taiping usage)
- **Repository or file**: `downloads/primary_texts/Ying_Jie_Gui_Zhen_1861.pdf` (26.4 MB facsimile PDF, Internet Archive)
- **Consultation status**: directly consulted (PDF acquired; facsimile and LOC catalogue record checked)
- **Source type**: Taiping primary
- **Reliability tier**: 1 (as evidence of Taiping thought)
- **Known bias**: Taiping official propaganda; presents the movement's religious and political claims as revealed truth. The dialogue format (between a Taiping official and a Qing loyalist who converts) is designed to refute criticisms and demonstrate Taiping ideological superiority.
- **Main contribution**: One of the few surviving complete Taiping printed texts. Provides direct evidence of Taiping self-representation: how the movement explained its theology, its break with Qing orthodoxy, its view of history, its anti-Manchu rationale, and its claims to legitimate rule. Uses a question-and-answer format covering cosmology, history, scripture, ritual, and political legitimacy.
- **Known problems**: As propaganda, it presents an idealised version of Taiping beliefs and a caricature of Qing loyalist objections. Cannot be used as evidence of actual administrative practice. The intended audience and circulation are uncertain.

- **English translation**: Veritable Records of the Qing Dynasty (or: Qing Veritable Records)
- **Author/editor/compiler**: Compiled by the Qing court; multiple reigns (Daoguang, Xianfeng, Tongzhi relevant to the Taiping)
- **Date of composition**: Compiled shortly after each emperor's death; Daoguang 道光 (r. 1820-1850), Xianfeng 咸丰 (r. 1850-1861), Tongzhi 同治 (r. 1861-1875)

- **Genre**: State chronicle / imperial record
- **Language**: Chinese (classical bureaucratic)
- **Repository or file**: FHAC portal at https://fhac.com.cn/index.html; portions digitised; full set identified but not fully downloaded
- **Consultation status**: identified but not directly consulted (FHAC portal accessed; specific reign sections for the Taiping period not yet systematically examined)
- **Source type**: Qing state
- **Reliability tier**: 2 (official state record; intentionally curated)
- **Known bias**: The Qing Veritable Records are the official chronicle of each emperor's reign, compiled posthumously. They were edited to present the emperor and his officials in the best possible light. Military defeats are downplayed; internal court conflicts are elided. The Taiping are referred to with standard derogatory terms (粤匪, etc.). However, the Records incorporate verbatim memorials and edicts that contain valuable information.
- **Main contribution**: Provides the official Qing timeline of the rebellion as it was meant to be remembered. Records: imperial edicts declaring Hong Xiuquan an outlaw; promotions and demotions of officials involved in suppression; military campaign summaries; rewards for loyal service; and the official narrative of each major battle from the Qing perspective.
- **Known problems**: The Records are massive and the Taiping-related entries are scattered across multiple reigns. The compiled nature means that information is often at second remove from original memorials. The Records were subsequently used as a source by the compilers of the 清史稿 (Draft Qing History), creating circular chains of citation.
- **Related wiki pages**: Qing court politics; Daoguang reign; Xianfeng reign; Tongzhi reign; Qing military response
- **Usable excerpts**: Imperial edicts of 1851-1853 responding to the initial rebellion; the court's response to the Nanjing proclamation; edicts authorising Zeng Guofan's expanded authority; the official record of the fall of Nanjing in 1864.
- **Claims supported**: That the Qing court initially underestimated the rebellion; that the court was slow to delegate military authority to Han Chinese officials; that the court's narrative of suppression emphasised loyal martyrdom.
- **Claims contradicted**: That the Qing court was monolithic in its response (there were significant factional disagreements).
- **Recommended citation form**: 《清实录》, 道光、咸丰、同治朝. Access via 中国第一历史档案馆 (FHAC) portal.

- **Claims contradicted**: That the Taiping simply "misunderstood" Christianity; that Taiping religion was merely a thin overlay on Chinese popular sectarianism.
