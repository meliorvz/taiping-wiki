## Design Context

### Users
Readers, researchers, students, and history-curious visitors using the Taiping wiki to understand the rebellion as a chronological story and as a source-rich reference archive. They need focused reading, quick navigation across many articles, and confidence that the material is organized and serious.

### Brand Personality
Editorial, scholarly, and dramatic. The interface should feel like a refined historical publication rather than a generic documentation site: calm enough for long reading, visually specific to nineteenth-century Chinese history, and memorable through typographic hierarchy, archival texture, and restrained motion.

### Aesthetic Direction
Long-form editorial web design inspired by the animation and chapter structure of the Everypact article reference: strong hero, sticky contents, smooth content reveals, rich callouts, and elegant navigation. The visual language should use Taiping-era Chinese identity signals such as traditional characters, seal-like red marks, warm paper tones, ink, jade, and muted campaign-map linework without becoming decorative clutter.

### Design Principles
- Keep the article as the primary experience on every screen.
- Make navigation dense but calm: searchable on desktop, touch-friendly and progressively disclosed on mobile.
- Use traditional Chinese typography signals sparingly and meaningfully, especially in the logo and hero.
- Prefer warm archival contrast, crisp borders, and restrained motion over generic gradients or card-heavy layouts.
- Preserve accessibility: readable body type, clear focus states, reduced-motion support, and no horizontal overflow across phone, tablet, and desktop.
